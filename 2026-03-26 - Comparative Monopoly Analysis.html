Synthesis — The Monopoly Overlap

All four share the defining characteristic: customers cannot leave. The mechanisms differ — IBM locks in through accumulated code, PFC through government mandate, FIH through geography, Amadeus through operational dependency — but the economic result is identical: near-zero churn, structural pricing power, and multi-decade duration. The key distinction is scale: IBM and Amadeus are global technology monopolies serving thousands of enterprises; PFC is a national financial monopoly serving an entire sector; FIH is a geographic monopoly serving 3,600 people. The investment returns come not from the monopoly itself (which is visible to anyone who looks) but from the market's failure to price it correctly.

The PFC Exception — Why Category Discount ≠ Conglomerate Discount

PFC breaks the pattern. It is not a monopoly hidden inside a conglomerate — it is the monopoly, fully visible, with no dilutive segments. The mispricing comes from a different source: category bias. Indian PSU stocks were systematically avoided by Western institutional investors regardless of underlying quality. PFC at 0.37× P/B with 20% ROE was mispriced not because analysts couldn't see the franchise, but because the category label ("Indian PSU") triggered automatic avoidance. This is important because it shows Andrew's framework is broader than "find hidden monopolies in conglomerates." The framework is: find durable franchises where the market's classification system creates a persistent discount to intrinsic value. The classification error can be structural (IBM, FIH, Amadeus) or categorical (PFC). The investment discipline is the same.

The Honest Assessment

Amadeus is the weakest of the four on crisis resilience and earnings stability. IBM's mainframe, PFC's loan book, and FIH's geographic monopoly all generate revenue regardless of economic conditions. Amadeus does not — its revenue is per-booking, and bookings collapse when people stop flying. This is the fundamental tension in the thesis: Amadeus is a monopoly with cyclical revenue. The franchise (zero churn, infinite switching costs) is as durable as any of the other three. But the earnings stream is more volatile. For a patient, concentrated investor like Andrew, this means larger drawdowns to sit through and lower Sharpe ratio on the same underlying quality. The PFC case study shows Andrew can hold through a 14% ROE trough. The question is whether he can hold Amadeus through a –12% ROE trough. The honest answer: it depends on entry price. At €55–58 (our calculated floor), the margin of safety absorbs a COVID repeat. At €68, it doesn't.
