The Verdict: 15 Monopolies, One Framework

Average monopoly score across all 15 holdings: 6.7 / 8. Every single investment Andrew was involved in — whether through his father (IBM), through WindAcre (Nielsen, TransDigm, Moody's, FNF, Alphabet, Amazon, ASML, TSMC, SAP, PFC, Expedia), or through his own idea generation (FIH, Amadeus) — is a monopoly or oligopoly. There are no exceptions. Zero.

The highest-scoring holdings are IBM (8), TransDigm (8), and Amadeus (7) — all three share the same defining characteristic: mission-critical infrastructure with regulatory/operational lock-in that makes switching existentially risky. This is Andrew's sweet spot. Not just "good businesses" or "quality compounders" — specifically: systems so embedded in their customers' operations that removal would cause operational failure.

The two lowest scorers — FIH (6) and Expedia (4) — diverge for opposite reasons. FIH has the strongest monopoly of any holding (174-year geographic lock-in, unbreakable) but fails on institutional scale. Expedia is the weakest monopoly (oligopoly with soft lock-in) but was initiated as a value chain play — buying the traffic to understand the toll road. Neither is a mistake. Both reveal different aspects of how Andrew thinks.

Amadeus at 7/8 matches the core WindAcre holdings (Nielsen, Moody's, FNF, Alphabet, Amazon). Its only weakness — cyclical earnings — is shared by FNF (real estate cycle) and Expedia (travel cycle). Andrew has demonstrated he can hold cyclical monopolies. The question is whether Amadeus's COVID trough (–12% ROE) is deeper than anything he's sat through before. The answer: PFC at 0.17× P/B (FY2020) was arguably more painful to hold. He held.

The Nielsen Campaign — What It Reveals About Andrew's Conviction

WindAcre accumulated 27.3% of Nielsen Holdings — the single most concentrated public equity position of any fund in 2022. Andrew was there for the entire campaign.
THE THESIS
Nielsen was the monopoly measurement standard for a $70B TV advertising market. Every ad buy, every upfront, every network valuation was denominated in Nielsen ratings. No alternative existed at scale. WindAcre's thesis: Nielsen ONE (cross-platform measurement, combining linear TV + streaming) would cement the monopoly in the digital era — making it more valuable, not less. Amin argued intrinsic value was $40–50/share with a credible path to $80 by 2025. The $28/share bid from Elliott/Brookfield valued a monopoly at a discount to its own infrastructure replacement cost.
THE CAMPAIGN
Q1 2015: WindAcre initiates Nielsen position.
2015–2021: Builds stake over 7 years. Patient accumulation.
Mar 2022: Elliott/Brookfield bid $28/share (~$16B incl. debt).
Apr 2022: WindAcre at 27.3% — enough to block UK scheme (requires 75%).
Apr 2022: Amin demands additional $1.1B in equity (~$40/share effective price). Rejected.
Aug 2022: Resolution — WindAcre joins the consortium. Receives $28/share for public shares + $500M equity commitment in private holdco with minority protections.
Oct 2022: Deal closes. Nielsen delisted.

What Nielsen Tells Us About How Tintern Will Invest

The Nielsen campaign reveals three things about Andrew's (and by extension, Tintern's) investment approach:

1. Conviction without limits. 27.3% of a single company is not a position. It's a statement of total conviction. WindAcre put more capital into Nielsen alone than most funds put into their entire portfolio. This only makes sense if you believe — with absolute certainty — that you understand the business better than the market. Andrew watched Amin do this. He will do it at Tintern.

2. Willingness to go activist when the monopoly is mispriced. WindAcre was not a passive holder at Nielsen. They blocked a deal, demanded better terms, and ultimately negotiated entry into the private consortium. This is the TCI lineage in action — Hohn taught Amin that if you own enough of a monopoly, you have leverage. Amin demonstrated it at Nielsen. Andrew will apply it at Tintern. For Amadeus: even a 0.5–1% stake, combined with a credible SOTP letter, could be the first shareholder voice the passive register has ever heard.

3. The outcome was not a clean win. Amin argued Nielsen was worth $40–80. It sold for $28. WindAcre negotiated the best deal available ($500M equity in the holdco with minority protections) but did not get the price they believed was fair. This matters for Joe: Andrew has seen a monopoly thesis fail to reprice in public markets and get taken private at a discount. He knows the risk. The Amadeus pitch should acknowledge it — "the conglomerate discount can persist, or worse, attract a PE bid below intrinsic value. The defence is the SOTP work that makes the discount visible before a predator arrives."
