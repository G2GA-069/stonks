Quality Tier Deep Dive

Each segment scored on five durability dimensions — the investment case hinges on which tier you weight most

We classify each business line into one of three quality tiers based on its structural durability under an AI disruption scenario. The key question for each segment: if agentic AI travel planners capture 20-30% of booking flows over the next decade, does this segment's revenue survive intact? The answer varies dramatically — from "completely immune" (Air IT) to "directly in the firing line" (Air Distribution). The tier you weight most in your valuation determines whether Amadeus at €68 is cheap, fair, or expensive.

SOTP Multiple Derivation — Rigorous Justification

Each tier's multiple is anchored in comparable transactions, public comps, and intrinsic frameworks

Assigning multiples to a SOTP without justification is hand-waving. The analysis below triangulates each tier's EBIT multiple through three lenses: (1) public comparable companies with similar business characteristics, (2) precedent transactions in adjacent verticals, and (3) a DCF-implied multiple based on each tier's growth, margin, and reinvestment profile. The goal is not false precision — it's to demonstrate that 25-30x for fortress earnings, 20-25x for durable growth, and 12-15x for exposed distribution are not arbitrary but sit comfortably within defensible ranges derived from market evidence.

Tier 1 — Fortress (Air IT / Altéa PSS): 25-30x EBIT
Mission-critical infrastructure SaaS with 5-10yr contracts, >99% retention, 37% EBIT margins, ~60% recurring

The Altéa PSS sits in a category of software businesses that essentially become operating systems for their customers. Airlines cannot function without their PSS — it controls departure control, ticketing, inventory management, and revenue integrity. The switching cost is not just financial ($50-100m migration cost for a large airline); it is existential — a botched PSS migration can ground flights. This creates a competitive moat comparable to the best vertical market software businesses globally. The appropriate comp set is not "travel tech" but rather "mission-critical vertical SaaS with extreme lock-in."

Tier 2 — Durable (Hospitality IT + Payments): 20-25x EBIT
High-growth platforms (15-20% revenue CAGR), expanding margins (22% → 30%+), structural tailwinds

Hospitality IT (TravelClick, iHotelier, HotSOS) and Payments are in the investment phase — below group-average margins today but growing significantly faster than the core. Hospitality IT benefits from the hotel industry's belated digitisation: independent hotels are ~5 years behind airlines in technology adoption, and Amadeus is the largest pure-play hospitality tech platform outside Oracle. Payments is a classic platform play — processing travel payments end-to-end with cross-sell into the existing airline and hotel base. The comp set here is high-growth vertical SaaS and payments platforms.

Tier 3 — Exposed (Air Distribution): 12-15x EBIT
Mature distribution platform, high margins (28%) but secular volume risk from AI disintermediation

Air Distribution is a toll-road business: Amadeus earns a booking fee (~€3.50-4.50 per segment) each time a travel agent or OTA books a flight through the GDS. The model is highly profitable but faces existential questions from AI-driven direct channel proliferation, agentic booking engines, and airline NDC strategies that bypass the GDS entirely. The comp set is mature distribution and network businesses facing disruption — not growing SaaS. The multiple should reflect the probability-weighted terminal value of cash flows that may be permanently impaired.

Multiple Derivation: Synthesis

The three-tier SOTP framework produces a midpoint equity value of €85/share versus a current market price of €68. But this €17 "gap" is not alpha — it's a statement about how the market prices the distribution risk. At €68, the market is implicitly assigning ~4.3x EBIT to Air Distribution (after subtracting the fortress and durable valuations and net debt). That's a deeply distressed multiple — below Western Union, below legacy media. Either the market is pricing in a >50% EBIT decline in distribution, or the fortress is being valued below our 27.5x midpoint, or some combination of both.

The intellectual honesty of the SOTP lies not in the point estimate but in what it reveals about implied beliefs. If you buy at €68 and the fortress alone is worth €47, you are paying just €21/share for the durable + exposed tiers combined. That's €9.3bn of implied EV for €1,069m of EBIT — about 8.7x blended. The market is pricing Amadeus as if roughly half its earnings base will be permanently impaired. Whether that's too pessimistic depends entirely on the pace and depth of AI-driven disintermediation over the next 5-10 years.

The Investment Case in Full

Start with what you own. At €68 per share, you are paying €30.2bn of market capitalisation — €32.4bn enterprise value — for a business generating €1,919m of EBIT across four segments of materially different quality. The question is not whether Amadeus is a good business. It is. The question is whether the market is paying you for the risk embedded in the revenue mix.

The portfolio is split roughly 55/45 between revenue that is structurally immune to AI disintermediation (Tier 1 + Tier 2: €3,587m revenue, €1,099m EBIT) and revenue that sits directly in the crosshairs (Tier 3: €2,930m revenue, €820m EBIT). That's the entire debate in two numbers.

The Altéa PSS is the crown jewel — 44% of group EBIT at a 37% margin, locked in on 5–10 year contracts with 200+ airlines, protected by switching costs that would make an enterprise SaaS company weep. An airline migrating off Altéa is making a $50–100M, multi-year bet that risks operational disruption to its entire departure control, ticketing, and inventory management stack. They don't do it lightly. Three airlines have attempted it in the last decade; one abandoned mid-migration.

The fortress earnings alone — valued at 27.5x midpoint — are worth €47 per share. Add the durable segments (Hospitality IT + Payments, growing mid-to-high teens, margins expanding toward 30%+) at 22.5x, and you get to €60 per share before a single booking fee crosses the GDS. That's your downside floor. At €68, you are paying just €8 per share — roughly €3.5bn of implied enterprise value — for the entire Air Distribution franchise.

Is €3.5bn cheap for a segment generating €820m of EBIT? At face value, yes — that's 4.3x EBIT, which prices it as a terminal value asset. But the operating leverage trap is real. Air Distribution's cost base is ~60% fixed. A 20% volume decline doesn't produce a 20% EBIT decline; it produces a 50–65% EBIT decline. If AI-driven disintermediation erodes 20% of GDS volumes by 2032 (our base case), Tier 3 EBIT drops from €820m to roughly €300–400m. At 10x impaired earnings, that's worth €3–4bn — or about €7–9 per share. The current price has this approximately right.

The bear case is not that Air Distribution goes to zero. It's that it enters a slow, margin-compressing decline that the market continually re-rates downward — death by a thousand cuts to the multiple. The bull case is that Amadeus successfully pivots Air Distribution into an "AI backbone" role (MCP translators, agentic APIs, content aggregation-as-a-service), converting a per-booking toll into an infrastructure fee. This is plausible but unproven.

Bottom line: At €68, you are buying the fortress at a reasonable price and getting Air Distribution for roughly its impaired terminal value. There is no margin of safety if distribution erodes faster than base case. There is meaningful upside if Tier 2 margins expand and the AI backbone pivot works. The stock is fairly priced for a realistic bear case but offers no compensation for tail risk. For a 20% IRR, you need €34–40 entry — which requires ~11% volume growth or, more realistically, a market sell-off that prices Amadeus like a capital goods company rather than a software compounder.

Hypothetical Merger & Separation Scenarios

What if you merged only the "good" parts and separated the "bad" parts?

The strategic thought experiment: what value could be unlocked if Amadeus and Travelport's fortress and durable segments were combined, while the exposed distribution businesses were merged separately or divested? This is the kind of analysis a GP like Apollo or Elliott would run when evaluating a transformative deal. The logic is straightforward: (1) the combined IT/PSS franchise would have near-monopoly status in airline technology, commanding premium multiples; (2) the combined distribution entity would have scale advantages to invest in the AI pivot; (3) antitrust is the binding constraint.

Combine Amadeus's Air IT (Altéa PSS), Hospitality IT, Payments, and Travelport's IT Solutions into a single entity focused on mission-critical travel technology. This "AeraTech" would be the undisputed global leader in airline PSS, hospitality tech, and travel payments — with no distribution exposure.

Antitrust Analysis: The Binding Constraint

Any combination of Amadeus and Travelport triggers immediate scrutiny from the European Commission (DG COMP) and the US Department of Justice (DOJ). The GDS market is a textbook oligopoly: three players (Amadeus ~43%, Sabre ~35%, Travelport ~22%) control ~97% of global air distribution. A merger reducing the market from three players to two would face extraordinary regulatory hurdles.

Scenario A (IT/PSS merger) is more defensible. The airline PSS market is distinct from GDS distribution. Amadeus (Altéa) and Travelport's legacy PSS serve different customer segments — Amadeus targets large full-service carriers while Travelport's IT serves smaller regional airlines. The combined share in PSS would be ~55-60%, which is high but potentially defensible given the competitive constraint from IBS Software, Hitit, and the emerging cloud-native entrants (Kiu, Radixx). Regulators would likely require behavioural remedies (interoperability commitments, data portability) rather than structural remedies.

Scenario B (Distribution merger) faces a near-certain block in its pure form. Combining Amadeus and Travelport's GDS operations would create a ~65% global market share entity in air distribution — far above typical concentration thresholds. Regulators blocked or required extensive remedies in far less concentrated deals (e.g., Sabre/Farelogix 2020, blocked by UK CMA). However, there are nuances that could shift the analysis:

1. Market definition is evolving. If regulators accept that Google Flights, airline direct channels, and AI-powered booking agents are effective competitive constraints on GDS pricing, the "relevant market" expands well beyond the traditional GDS triopoly. A combined 65% of GDS bookings might represent only 40-45% of total air distribution if direct channels are included — still high, but more defensible.

2. Failing firm defence. If Travelport's debt burden is unsustainable (6-7x leverage with declining volumes), Elliott might argue that Travelport's GDS business would exit the market absent a merger — making the counterfactual a duopoly (Amadeus + Sabre) regardless. This defence has succeeded in other industries (e.g., Lloyds/HBOS 2008) but is rarely accepted in tech.

3. Structural remedies. The most likely path to approval would involve divesting one of the legacy GDS brands (e.g., selling Worldspan or Galileo to a new entrant or existing competitor) to maintain three-player competition. Google, Amazon, or a travel-tech startup might be interested buyers for a €2-3bn GDS business as a beachhead for AI-driven distribution.

Bottom line: A full Amadeus-Travelport merger is almost certainly blocked without significant divestitures. The more realistic scenario is a targeted asset deal: Amadeus acquires Travelport's IT/PSS assets (defensible, limited overlap) and leaves the distribution business to Elliott for a separate process (PE sale, IPO, or merger with Sabre's distribution arm). This "cherry-pick" approach captures most of the value creation from Scenario A (~€2-3bn of synergy value) while avoiding the regulatory minefield of Scenario B.
