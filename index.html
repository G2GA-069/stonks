Revenue & Profit Composition

Four business lines with materially different quality characteristics — the doughnut tells the portfolio story at a glance

Amadeus reports two segments — Air Distribution and IT Solutions — but this masks a critical divergence in revenue quality. We decompose IT Solutions further into Air IT (the Altéa PSS platform), Hospitality IT (TravelClick, iHotelier, HotSOS), and Payments/New Businesses. Each line has fundamentally different switching costs, cyclicality, contract structure, and exposure to AI-driven disintermediation. The doughnut charts below show how the revenue and EBIT mix shifts when you look through the reporting segments to the economic reality.

Quality Tier Deep Dive

Each segment scored on five durability dimensions — the investment case hinges on which tier you weight most

We classify each business line into one of three quality tiers based on its structural durability under an AI disruption scenario. The key question for each segment: if agentic AI travel planners capture 20-30% of booking flows over the next decade, does this segment's revenue survive intact? The answer varies dramatically — from "completely immune" (Air IT) to "directly in the firing line" (Air Distribution). The tier you weight most in your valuation determines whether Amadeus at €68 is cheap, fair, or expensive.

Airlines need a Passenger Service System regardless of whether the booking originates from a GDS, NDC pipe, AI agent, or airline.com. Altéa serves 200+ airlines globally. A PSS migration takes 18–36 months, costs $50–100M+, and carries acute operational risk — you are swapping the engine mid-flight. Contracts run 5–10 years with per-passenger-boarded economics that grow with traffic, not distribution channel. No AI agent can disintermediate the PSS. It is the operational backbone.

Hotel PMS/CRS (TravelClick, iHotelier, HotSOS) and B2B payments infrastructure are operational systems, not distribution. A hotel property management system doesn't care whether the guest booked via Booking.com, an AI agent, or walked in off the street. Payments is a pure toll on transaction flow — it grows with travel volume regardless of distribution channel. Both segments are sub-scale on margins today (19%); at maturity, 30–35% EBIT margins are achievable as the fixed-cost platform scales. This is where the organic growth story lives: hospitality IT is growing mid-teens, payments high-teens.

Pure per-booking toll at ~€3–5 per segment. Every transaction routed via AI-direct, NDC-direct, or an airline chatbot is a booking permanently lost to Amadeus distribution. Contracts are volume-based with limited guaranteed minimums — when volumes decline, revenue declines linearly while costs stay fixed. Google AI Mode (live beta, 2026), Sabre/PayPal/MindTrip (Q2 2026), and airline-direct AI agents are all attacking this layer. If AI-direct achieves >10% conversion parity with GDS-mediated booking flows, supplier migration will accelerate non-linearly. We estimate this threshold is 18–30 months away.

The Investment Case in Full

Start with what you own. At €68 per share, you are paying €30.2bn of market capitalisation — €32.4bn enterprise value — for a business generating €1,919m of EBIT across four segments of materially different quality. The question is not whether Amadeus is a good business. It is. The question is whether the market is paying you for the risk embedded in the revenue mix.

The portfolio is split roughly 55/45 between revenue that is structurally immune to AI disintermediation (Tier 1 + Tier 2: €3,587m revenue, €1,099m EBIT) and revenue that sits directly in the crosshairs (Tier 3: €2,930m revenue, €820m EBIT). That's the entire debate in two numbers.

The Altéa PSS is the crown jewel — 44% of group EBIT at a 37% margin, locked in on 5–10 year contracts with 200+ airlines, protected by switching costs that would make an enterprise SaaS company weep. An airline migrating off Altéa is making a $50–100M, multi-year bet that risks operational disruption to its entire departure control, ticketing, and inventory management stack. They don't do it lightly. Three airlines have attempted it in the last decade; one abandoned mid-migration.

The fortress earnings alone — valued at 27.5x midpoint — are worth €47 per share. Add the durable segments (Hospitality IT + Payments, growing mid-to-high teens, margins expanding toward 30%+) at 22.5x, and you get to €60 per share before a single booking fee crosses the GDS. That's your downside floor. At €68, you are paying just €8 per share — roughly €3.5bn of implied enterprise value — for the entire Air Distribution franchise.

Is €3.5bn cheap for a segment generating €820m of EBIT? At face value, yes — that's 4.3x EBIT, which prices it as a terminal value asset. But the operating leverage trap is real. Air Distribution's cost base is ~60% fixed. A 20% volume decline doesn't produce a 20% EBIT decline; it produces a 50–65% EBIT decline. If AI-driven disintermediation erodes 20% of GDS volumes by 2032 (our base case), Tier 3 EBIT drops from €820m to roughly €300–400m. At 10x impaired earnings, that's worth €3–4bn — or about €7–9 per share. The current price has this approximately right.

The bear case is not that Air Distribution goes to zero. It's that it enters a slow, margin-compressing decline that the market continually re-rates downward — death by a thousand cuts to the multiple. The bull case is that Amadeus successfully pivots Air Distribution into an "AI backbone" role (MCP translators, agentic APIs, content aggregation-as-a-service), converting a per-booking toll into an infrastructure fee. This is plausible but unproven.

Bottom line: At €68, you are buying the fortress at a reasonable price and getting Air Distribution for roughly its impaired terminal value. There is no margin of safety if distribution erodes faster than base case. There is meaningful upside if Tier 2 margins expand and the AI backbone pivot works. The stock is fairly priced for a realistic bear case but offers no compensation for tail risk. For a 20% IRR, you need €34–40 entry — which requires ~11% volume growth or, more realistically, a market sell-off that prices Amadeus like a capital goods company rather than a software compounder.
